# Flea-, Louse-, and Mite-Borne Disease Disposition Engine Evidence Synthesis

Insect and mite vectors cause distinct clusters of disease.  Fleas (on rodents or pets) can transmit murine typhus and plague, often in urban or endemic rural areas【49†L109-L117】【64†L231-L239】.  Lice (body lice) thrive in crowded, unhygienic conditions and can spread epidemic typhus, trench fever, and louse-borne relapsing fever【51†L109-L117】【53†L129-L135】.  Mites (scabies mites or chiggers) cause scabies and scrub typhus; scabies yields intensely itchy rash, especially in dormitories, while scrub typhus (chiggers) causes fever with an eschar【57†L114-L122】【59†L125-L133】. Early recognition of severe bites or rashes (e.g. an eschar) is crucial【59†L125-L133】【64†L231-L239】.

Lice spread by close contact and poor hygiene; outbreaks of epidemic typhus have occurred in refugee camps, prisons, and among the homeless【51†L109-L117】.  In such settings, fever and rash warrant urgent care (Tier 1) and doxycycline treatment【51†L147-L154】【51†L179-L187】. Louse-borne relapsing fever (Borrelia recurrentis) also arises in overcrowding, causing recurrent fevers【53†L84-L90】【53†L123-L130】.

For example, scrub typhus (chigger bite) typically produces a fever and a dark eschar at the bite site【59†L125-L133】.  Finding an eschar in a traveler or someone with fever strongly suggests scrub typhus or other rickettsioses and should prompt immediate evaluation and doxycycline therapy【59†L125-L133】【64†L231-L239】.  Early antibiotic treatment is lifesaving in these diseases.

*Drivers:* Fever or systemic signs after flea/lice exposure greatly escalate urgency【49†L131-L139】【51†L147-L154】. Purely local skin symptoms (itchy bites/rash) with no fever are generally low-risk (self-care) unless in a known outbreak setting.

## Output 6: Fail-Safe Rules

- **Any fever after exposure in endemic regions → escalate.** For example, **fever after travel to a typhus/scrub typhus area** (Asia, Africa, Southern US) triggers urgent care【59†L125-L133】【53†L108-L115】.
- **Neurologic or hemodynamic signs → emergency.** Confusion, seizures, hypotension, or respiratory distress after a vector exposure suggest severe typhus or plague septicemia【51†L147-L154】【64†L258-L266】.  These always default to Tier 1 regardless of confidence.
- **Widespread rash with hypotension → emergency.** Suggests epidemic typhus or septicemic plague; immediate hospitalization and doxy.
- **Eschar + systemic symptoms** is always concerning. If an eschar is found with fever/headache, assume scrub typhus (doxy) (Tier 2)【59†L125-L133】.
- **Infestation uncertainty in high-risk context**: Even if fever absent, suspected lice/scabies outbreak should prompt medical review (Tier 3) to prevent spread (treat contacts).
- **Pulmonary symptoms + rodent contact → escalate.** Any cough or pneumonia after rodent/flea exposure must consider pneumonic plague (Tier 1).
- **Pregnancy or young age**: Lower threshold for evaluation in pregnant women or infants after exposure (e.g. fever and rash in pregnant traveler → Tier 2), due to higher risk for complications (LBRF causes fetal loss【53†L117-L125】).
- **Any doubt** in a high-risk scenario leads to escalation. The system biases towards safety: if uncertain about travel region or duration, or tick/lice identification, it errs on the side of “seek care.”

These fail-safes ensure critical cases are not missed. For instance, CDC recommends immediate doxy for any suspected epidemic typhus without waiting for confirmation【51†L179-L187】; likewise, we require empirical action on key presentations.
