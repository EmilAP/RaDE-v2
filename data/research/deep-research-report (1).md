# Tick-Borne Disease Disposition Engine Evidence Synthesis

Ticks (e.g. *Ixodes scapularis*, blacklegged/deer tick) are small (3–4 mm) blood‑feeding arthropods.  Adult females feed on hosts for 4–5 days until engorged【42†L193-L198】【37†L215-L219】. 【43†embed_image】Ticks must be attached for many hours (typically ≥36 h) before pathogens like *Borrelia burgdorferi* are transmitted【47†L25-L28】【42†L193-L198】.  A flat (non-engorged) tick, or one removed quickly, poses negligible risk【47†L25-L28】. 

Lyme disease often begins with an erythema migrans “bull’s-eye” rash on the skin. 【46†embed_image】For example, an expanding red rash at a tick bite site is pathognomonic for Lyme disease【45†L138-L141】.  Recognizing this rash is important: it typically appears days to weeks after exposure and should prompt prompt outpatient evaluation (Tier 3).

| **Brief attachment (<36 h, not engorged)**    | Endemic area (e.g. Northeast US, parts of Canada) | Possible Lyme (but unlikely unless ≥36 h); very low risk others                            | Low risk; *Borrelia* transmission unlikely if <36 h【47†L25-L28】.            |
| **Prolonged attachment (≥36 h, partially engorged)** | Endemic area                    | High risk Lyme; moderate for *Anaplasma*/*Ehrlichia*; possible *Babesia* (if *Ixodes*)    | Moderate risk; prophylactic doxy recommended (Lyme)【47†L25-L28】【4†L37-L44】. |
| **Fully engorged tick (attached days)**       | Endemic area                        | Very high risk Lyme; high for *Anaplasma/Ehrlichia*; Babesia risk; rickettsiae (if Dermacentor) | High risk; disease likely transmitted【47†L25-L28】【4†L37-L44】.               |

*Evidence:* Transmission of *B. burgdorferi* (Lyme) is negligible if tick feed <36 h【47†L25-L28】, especially if the tick appears flat【47†L25-L28】.  Ticks engorged with blood imply ≥2–3 days attachment, greatly raising Lyme risk【47†L25-L28】【4†L37-L44】.  Rickettsial pathogens (e.g. RMSF) have no practical PEP; infection depends on regional vector presence and symptoms【4†L63-L67】【16†L114-L122】.  Geographic endemicity influences hazards (e.g. North America: Lyme, anaplasmosis; Europe/Asia: TBE, etc.).

## Output 2: Prophylaxis Decision Matrix  

| **Disease/Hazard**        | **Post-Exposure Prophylaxis**         | **Criteria/Timing**                                  | **Notes / Evidence**                               |
|---------------------------|---------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| **Lyme disease** (*Borrelia burgdorferi*) | Yes – single-dose doxycycline | Tick bite by an *Ixodes* tick in high-incidence area, attached ≥36 h, and within 72 h of removal【4†L37-L44】. | Significantly reduces Lyme risk【47†L40-L43】. Safe for all ages【4†L37-L44】. |
| **Anaplasmosis / Ehrlichiosis** | No specific PEP | – | No proven prophylaxis; treat empirically with doxycycline if fever develops【4†L63-L67】. |
| **Babesiosis** | No PEP | – | No PEP; monitor for symptoms (fatigue, dark urine) especially in asplenic/elderly【14†L126-L133】. |
| **Rickettsial diseases** (e.g. RMSF and other spotted fevers) | No PEP | – | No PEP; begin doxycycline immediately if symptoms arise【16†L114-L122】. |
| **Tularemia** (*Francisella tularensis*) | No PEP | – | No general PEP for tick exposure; instruct patient to seek care if fever/ulcer develops. Treat with streptomycin/fluoroquinolone/doxycycline if diagnosed【23†L13-L16】. |
| **Tick-borne encephalitis (TBE)** | Pre-exposure vaccine in endemic regions; no PEP | – | Vaccine available and recommended for travelers to endemic areas【18†L135-L138】. No antiviral PEP; treat CNS involvement supportively【18†L173-L178】. |
| **Powassan virus** | No PEP | – | No vaccine/meds; supportive care if sick. Outbreaks rare (Northeast US/Great Lakes)【20†L119-L127】. |
| **Relapsing fever (Borrelia miyamotoi)** | No PEP | – | No PEP; diagnosis by blood smear or PCR if fever occurs ~2 weeks post‑bite【25†L109-L117】. Treat with antibiotics if needed. |
| **Crimean–Congo hemorrhagic fever (CCHF)** | Ribavirin (investigational) | – | No licensed PEP; ribavirin has been used experimentally after high-risk exposures (e.g. needlestick)【8†L432-L440】, but effectiveness is unproven. Emphasize tick avoidance. |

*Evidence:* Only Lyme disease has evidence-based antibiotic PEP (single-dose doxycycline within 72 h)【4†L37-L44】【47†L40-L43】.  No routine antibiotic PEP is recommended for anaplasmosis, babesiosis, ehrlichiosis, or RMSF【4†L63-L67】.  CCHF and TBE have no established PEP; CCHF ribavirin PEP is unproven【8†L432-L440】, and TBE prevention relies on vaccination【18†L135-L138】.  Tularemia prophylaxis is generally not used for tick exposure (treatment is for clinical disease)【23†L13-L16】.

## Output 3: Rash/Symptom Urgency Matrix  

| **Clinical Scenario**                     | **Triage Tier** | **Rationale**                                    |
|-------------------------------------------|-----------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| **Expanding erythema migrans rash** (Lyme) | Tier 3 (Routine) | Outpatient antibiotic therapy (doxycycline or amoxicillin); not emergent【45†L138-L141】. |
| **Fever in endemic area after tick bite** | Tier 2 (Urgent)  | Possible anaplasmosis/ehrlichiosis: treat empirically with doxycycline pending tests【12†L128-L133】【12†L133-L139】. |
| **Fever + headache/myalgia**             | Tier 2 (Urgent)  | Suggestive of anaplasmosis or ehrlichiosis (incubation 1–2 weeks)【12†L124-L130】; begin doxycycline promptly. |
| **Fever + petechial or purpuric rash**    | Tier 1 (Emergency)| Classic for RMSF or other rickettsiosis; high fatality if untreated【16†L114-L122】【16†L172-L179】. Urgent doxycycline and hospitalization. |
| **Neurologic signs (confusion, stiff neck)** | Tier 1 (Emergency) | Could indicate tickborne encephalitis or severe RMSF/Powassan. Rapid evaluation and supportive care are critical【20†L123-L130】【16†L114-L122】. |
| **Severe constitutional symptoms** (e.g. hypotension, ARDS) | Tier 1 (Emergency) | Suggests severe anaplasmosis/RMSF; immediate stabilization and IV antibiotics【16†L139-L146】【16†L114-L122】. |
| **Fever + jaundice or dark urine**        | Tier 2 (Urgent)  | Suggests babesiosis (hemolytic anemia)【14†L121-L130】. Requires prompt lab work (blood smear, PCR) and treatment if confirmed. |
| **Relapsing fever pattern (fever returns)** | Tier 2 (Urgent) | Borrelia miyamotoi may cause recurrent fevers. Evaluate and treat with antibiotics; fever episodes can be protracted【25†L109-L117】. |

*Evidence:* RMSF is rapidly progressive and often fatal without early treatment【16†L114-L122】; any suspicion (fever after tick in RMSF area) warrants Tier 1. Ehrlichiosis/anaplasmosis typically present with fever, headache ~1–2 weeks after a bite【12†L124-L130】; delayed treatment increases severity【12†L168-L176】, so Tier 2 is advised. Babesiosis can cause hemolysis in high‑risk patients【14†L126-L133】; dark urine or jaundice is worrisome. Powassan virus causes encephalitis (~10% fatality)【20†L123-L130】; neurological signs at any time -> Tier 1. Erythema migrans alone is treated non-emergently (Tier 3)【45†L138-L141】.

## Output 6: Fail-Safe / Escalation Rules  

- **Any severe symptom after tick bite → emergency (Tier 1).** Examples: confusion, seizures, difficulty breathing, hypotension, or multi-organ failure. These red flags (as seen in RMSF/Powassan) always override other considerations【16†L114-L122】【20†L123-L130】.
- **Fever in endemic setting → urgent care.** Any fever occurring within ~1–2 weeks after travel/exposure in a tickborne region (e.g. Northeast US for Lyme/Ehrlichia or West US for RMSF) should prompt medical evaluation (Tier 1–2) due to anaplasmosis and RMSF risk【12†L133-L139】【16†L114-L122】.
- **Unknown tick status in high-risk area → lean conservative.** If it’s unclear how long a tick was attached but exposure was in a high-incidence area (or tick found on skin in season), err on side of caution (consider Tier 2 and possibly prophylaxis)【47†L25-L28】【4†L37-L44】.
- **Neurologic symptoms trump low exposure risk.** Even if initial exposure seemed low-risk, any new neuro signs (e.g. severe headache, stiff neck, seizures) should be handled as Tier 1 (possible viral encephalitis).
- **Pregnancy with tick exposure/symptoms.** Pregnant people have altered risk/medication considerations (e.g. avoid doxycycline). Tier 2 is advised for any suspicious scenario (Lyme risk justifies testing/treatment despite pregnancy, but neurosigns mandate Tier 1).
- **Immunocompromise or asplenia.** In these hosts, lower thresholds apply: a routine fever or rash can escalate to Tier 2, given higher severity potential (babesiosis, ehrlichia)【14†L126-L133】【12†L180-L184】.
- **Tick removed improperly.** If a tick’s mouthparts are known to be retained or removal was traumatic, escalate to Tier 2 for assessment, since secondary infection risk or incomplete removal may complicate things.
- **Any uncertainty → escalate.** When in doubt, the engine defaults conservatively. For example, if the timeline of attachment is unclear and the area is known for tick-borne diseases, recommend clinical evaluation (Tier 2). 

Each rule prioritizes safety over omission. For instance, CDC guidance emphasizes treating RMSF on suspicion【16†L114-L122】; similarly, the engine should err on the side of “seek care” if a critical time window or symptom is involved.
